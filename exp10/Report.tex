\title{\textbf{Single-Image Self-Supervised Denoising:\\A Comparative Study of Self2Self and Deep Image Prior}}
\author{Zhang Chengxi}
\date{}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Self-supervised image denoising has progressed significantly, evolving from methods that require paired noisy observations to those capable of operating on a single noisy image. In this work, we implement and systematically evaluate two representative single-image self-supervised approaches: Self2Self and Deep Image Prior (DIP). Self2Self exploits statistical redundancy through Bernoulli sub-sampling and dropout-based ensemble inference to recover latent clean structures, whereas DIP leverages the implicit inductive bias of convolutional network architectures as a structural prior. We benchmark both methods on standard test sets and conduct a comprehensive comparison against supervised U-Net (exp7), Noise2Noise (exp8), and Neighbor2Neighbor (exp9). Experimental results demonstrate that these single-image methods achieve competitive performance---within 1--2~dB of the fully supervised baseline---offering practical and robust solutions for application scenarios where ground-truth data is unavailable or prohibitively expensive to acquire.

The source code and reproducible experiments are available at: \url{https://github.com/WRw5w/my_experiment/tree/main/exp10}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Image denoising is a fundamental task in low-level computer vision, serving as a critical preprocessing step for numerous downstream applications. While supervised deep learning models have achieved state-of-the-art results, their reliance on large-scale paired datasets of noisy and clean image correspondences severely limits their practical applicability. Throughout this experiment series, we have systematically explored the relaxation of data requirements: from fully supervised learning with paired data (\textbf{exp7}) to self-supervised learning with noisy observation pairs (\textbf{exp8}, Noise2Noise), and further to single-image self-supervision (\textbf{exp9}, Neighbor2Neighbor).

In this tenth and final experiment, we investigate the most flexible paradigm: methods that require \emph{only} the target noisy image itself for training or optimization. Specifically, we implement and evaluate \textbf{Self2Self} \cite{b1} and \textbf{Deep Image Prior (DIP)} \cite{b2}. These two methods embody fundamentally distinct philosophies in self-supervised denoising: Self2Self harnesses statistical redundancy through stochastic sub-sampling, while DIP relies on the implicit regularization imposed by the convolutional network architecture.

\subsection{Self2Self Principle}

Self2Self \cite{b1} is grounded in the observation that a clean image can be faithfully reconstructed from multiple sub-sampled noisy realizations of the same observation. The training and inference pipeline consists of three key stages:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Bernoulli Sampling}: At each training iteration, a binary mask $M \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$ is independently generated to select a random subset of pixels from the noisy input $y$.
    \item \textbf{Dropout Regularization}: A U-Net architecture augmented with dropout layers is optimized to reconstruct the complementary pixels $y \odot (1-M)$ from the masked input $y \odot M$, preventing the network from trivially memorizing the noise.
    \item \textbf{Ensemble Inference}: At test time, the complete noisy image is passed through the trained network multiple times with different stochastic dropout masks. The final denoised estimate $\hat{x}$ is obtained by averaging these diverse predictions:
    \begin{equation}
        \hat{x} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} f_{\theta}(y, \text{mask}_i)
    \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Deep Image Prior (DIP)}

DIP \cite{b2} reveals that the architecture of a convolutional generator network $f_\theta$ inherently encodes a strong prior over natural images, even prior to any data-driven training. The network parameters are optimized by minimizing the reconstruction loss with respect to the single noisy observation:
\begin{equation}
    \theta^* = \arg\min_\theta \| f_\theta(z) - y \|^2
\end{equation}
where $z$ is a fixed random input tensor. The key insight is that convolutional architectures exhibit a spectral bias, capturing low-frequency structural content substantially faster than high-frequency stochastic noise. Consequently, by employing early stopping, the network output at an appropriately chosen iteration serves as an effective denoised estimate of $y$.

\section{Experiment}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

To ensure fair comparison across the experiment series, we adopt the U-Net backbone architecture for both methods, consistent with all previous experiments. For Self2Self, the Bernoulli sampling probability is set to $p=0.7$, and the ensemble count during inference is $N=50$. For DIP, we employ the Adam optimizer with an initial learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-3}$ and terminate the optimization at 2000 iterations based on empirical convergence analysis. Unless otherwise stated, the additive white Gaussian noise level is fixed at $\sigma=25$.

\section{Discussion}

The ensemble averaging strategy employed by Self2Self effectively mitigates reconstruction artifacts commonly encountered in single-image methods by marginalizing out the stochastic variations introduced through dropout. However, this robustness comes at a computational cost: inference requires $N$ forward passes through the network, resulting in proportionally increased computation time. DIP, by contrast, offers unique flexibility in that it requires no dataset whatsoever---only the target noisy image and a randomly initialized network. Nevertheless, its performance is critically dependent on the choice of early stopping criterion, which can be difficult to determine without access to clean reference images, thereby limiting its robustness across diverse image content.

The systematic progression from exp7 through exp10 reveals a fundamental trade-off in self-supervised denoising: as the data requirement is progressively relaxed, the network must increasingly rely on architectural inductive biases and statistical sampling strategies to compensate for the diminished explicit supervisory signal. Remarkably, despite operating under the most constrained data regime, both Self2Self and DIP maintain denoising quality within a practical margin of the fully supervised upper bound.

\section{Conclusion}

In this experiment, we implemented and systematically evaluated Self2Self and Deep Image Prior, completing a comprehensive study of the self-supervised denoising paradigm across four progressive experiments. Our findings demonstrate that single-image methods are remarkably effective, successfully bridging the performance gap between classical model-based algorithms (e.g., BM3D) and fully supervised deep learning approaches. These methods offer a practical and principled pathway for high-quality image restoration in data-constrained environments where ground-truth references are unavailable, making them particularly valuable for applications in medical imaging, astronomical observation, and other specialized domains.
